Great question—and very important to get right early.

You want a disclaimer that:

* **protects you legally**
* **doesn’t scare users away**
* **fits naturally into your UX**

The key is:

> Be **clear**, **honest**, but **non-intrusive**

---

# 🧠 What You Need (3 Layers)

You should **not rely on just one disclaimer**.

You need:

## 1. ✅ Core Legal Disclaimer (full, formal)

## 2. 🧩 Contextual Micro Disclaimers (in product)

## 3. ⚙️ Terms of Service / Privacy Coverage

---

# 🛡️ 1. Core Legal Disclaimer (Required)

## Where:

* Onboarding (required acceptance)
* Settings → “Medical Disclaimer”
* Footer (optional web)

## Copy (use this as your base)

This application provides general wellness insights based on user-reported data. It is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any medical condition.

The information and recommendations provided by this app are for informational and educational purposes only and should not be considered medical advice.

Always consult with a qualified healthcare professional before making any decisions related to your health, diet, or treatment.

If you are experiencing severe or persistent symptoms, seek medical attention immediately.

---

# ⚡ 2. In-Product Micro Disclaimers (Critical for trust)

You don’t want users thinking:

> “This app KNOWS the truth”

Instead:

> “This app is helping me discover patterns”

## Where to place them

### A. On “Best Next Action”

Small, subtle line:

> “Based on your recent data patterns”

---

### B. On Insights

Use confidence language:

* “Possible pattern”
* “Likely association”
* “Emerging signal”

Avoid:

* “Causes”
* “Will result in”

---

### C. On Experiments (VERY IMPORTANT)

Add a small note:

> “This is a personal experiment, not medical guidance”

---

### D. On Weekly Intelligence

Add footer:

> “Insights are based on your logged data and may not reflect clinical conclusions”

---

# 🧪 3. Health Lab Specific Disclaimer

This is your highest-risk feature.

Add this in experiment start flow:

This experiment is designed to help you observe how your body responds to certain changes. It is not a medical treatment or diagnosis.

If you have existing health conditions or concerns, consult a healthcare professional before starting.

---

# 🚨 Things You MUST Avoid (Legally risky)

Never say:

❌ “This causes…”
❌ “This will fix…”
❌ “This treats…”
❌ “This diagnoses…”

Instead use:

✅ “may be linked to”
✅ “appears associated with”
✅ “you may benefit from”
✅ “based on your data”

---

# 📍 Placement Strategy (Best UX)

## Onboarding

* Show full disclaimer
* Require acceptance

## Daily use

* DO NOT show big disclaimers repeatedly
* Use subtle micro language instead

## Settings

* Full disclaimer accessible anytime

## Experiments

* Show short disclaimer on start

---

# 🧠 Tone Matters More Than Length

You don’t want this:

> ⚠️ WARNING THIS IS NOT MEDICAL ADVICE ⚠️

You want this:

> “Based on your data patterns…”

Smooth, confident, non-alarming.

---

# ⚖️ Risk Level Breakdown

| Feature         | Risk   | Mitigation           |
| --------------- | ------ | -------------------- |
| Recommendations | Medium | wording + onboarding |
| Insights        | Medium | confidence language  |
| Health Lab      | High   | explicit disclaimer  |
| Weekly          | Low    | soft disclaimer      |

---

# 🔥 Best Practice (High-end apps like Oura/Whoop)

They:

* **never sound medical**
* always say:

  * “based on your data”
  * “suggests”
  * “may help”

That’s the model you want.

---

# 🧠 Final Recommendation

Use:

## 1. Strong onboarding disclaimer (required)

## 2. Soft, contextual language everywhere else

## 3. Extra disclaimer at experiment start

That combination:

* protects you legally
* keeps UX clean
* builds trust instead of fear
